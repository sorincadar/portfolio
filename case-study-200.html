Chapter 01 — Introduction
Enterprise CIAM  ·  B2B2B Identity Platform  ·  Global

Rebuilding the Identity Layer for a Global B2B2B Ecosystem

Topcon is a global manufacturer of precision positioning technology for construction, surveying, and agriculture. As its software portfolio shifted from hardware-tied licenses to cloud subscriptions, the platform managing user identity could not support the model the business had already committed to.

The Problem — Compounding Failures on Two Fronts

Architectural limitations generated simultaneous pressure — operational costs for the business, and workflow failures for the people using the system daily.

Business Problems
User Problems
Architectural Mismatch

Authentication and authorization operated as disconnected systems. Deleting a user identity did not cascade to application license databases, permanently trapping paid software seats until engineering intervened manually.

Onboarding Dead Ends

Users registering without an invitation were placed in an isolated empty organization by default. No discovery path, no request mechanism, no route to their employer's actual environment without admin action.

No Digital Dealer Relationship

Topcon's commercial model relies on dealers as the primary customer interface. No formal digital structure existed for this relationship — no consent record, no audit trail, no scoped access, no revocation mechanism.

Dealer Credential Workarounds

The only available path for dealers to establish client access was a 17-step manual sequence involving creating accounts under the customer's own identity and self-assigning administrative rights. No formal alternative existed.

Enterprise Adoption Blocked

The platform had no federation capability. Enterprise IT departments require SSO integration with their own identity providers as a procurement condition, not a preference. Every enterprise contract stalling at this requirement was a blocked deal.

No Self-Service Controls

Users could not edit their own profiles, view active sessions, or remotely revoke device access. The organizational owner role was permanently tied to the original account creator — if they left, control was inaccessible without engineering intervention.

Database Fragmentation

Unverified free-text registration generated a new tenant for every company name variation typed. 14,800 zero-activity organizations accumulated, blocking automated billing infrastructure and requiring continuous engineering cleanup.

Flat Structure at Enterprise Scale

800 organizations each managing 200+ users were forced into a single undifferentiated list with no hierarchy, no divisions, and no structural separation between regions or business units.

The Solution

In the hardware era, software licenses were tied to machine serial numbers — user identity was irrelevant to authorization. The shift to cloud subscriptions changed that entirely: every license, every access decision, and every revocation became attached to a verified human account. Identity became the enforcement layer the system had to be built around.

The platform separates personal identity management from organizational administration through a persistent global context header. Five modules cover the full operational scope.

Module 01
User Management

· Invitation flows with mandatory profile assignment
· Gated join request queue, separated from the active directory
· Bulk onboarding

Research

A Two-Track Research Process: What to Build. How to Build It Correctly.

Research ran as two parallel streams with distinct purposes. Stakeholder-provided research established the mandates — technical requirements, commercial pressures, and operational failures the design had to address. Independent UX research built the foundation to execute those mandates correctly — through domain knowledge, competitive interaction intelligence, and behavioral depth extracted through proxy research.

Track 01 — Stakeholder Research

Defined what needed to be built
Source 01

Business Context Analysis

Non-negotiable outcomes:

  • Federation: Unblock enterprise IT procurement.
  • Centralized Permissions: End manual per-app admin.
  • Dealer Infrastructure: Documented consent & revocation.
  • Scalable Onboarding: Replace manual SaaS email invites.
Source 02

Quantitative Research

Quantified structural failures:

  • 4,700 duplicate orgs from unverified free-text registration.
  • 12% of invites ignored with no expiry or alerts.
  • 900 users in 20+ orgs, revealing dealers operating without formal access.

Data proved what broke; qualitative research was mandated to find out why.

Source 03

Support Ticket Analysis

Escalating operational debt:

  • 31% of identity tickets required engineering database access.
  • 100% escalation rate for trapped licenses and ownership transfers.
  • 3–7 days downtime for frozen seats and inaccessible subscriptions.
  • 5× ticket volume growth tracking SaaS expansion.
Source 04

Competitive Research Report

Market baseline & gaps:

  • Minimum requirements: Domain verification and centralized identity management are now industry standards.
  • The strategic gap: No competitor offered a formalized cross-tenant dealer access model. Dealers were treated as standard internal users everywhere.

Track 02 — UX Investigation

Built the foundation to execute mandates correctly

Stakeholder research defined what needed to be built. It could not define how to execute those requirements correctly — which required domain knowledge built before synthesis began, and behavioral intelligence that no existing document contained.

Source 05

CIAM Industry Research

Before synthesis could begin, the stakeholder documents required a conceptual foundation to interpret correctly. Each structural failure in the legacy system had a surface-level explanation that would have produced a wrong design response. Ghost organizations looked like a registration UI problem. Trapped licenses looked like a database bug. The dealer workaround looked like a security incident to block. Five conceptual unlocks corrected each misdiagnosis before it could take hold.

01
IAM vs. CIAM — The Wrong Architectural Model

The legacy platform applied Enterprise IAM (high-trust, bounded employees) to CIAM realities (unverified, self-registering users). Ghost organizations weren't UI bugs—they were the predictable output of the wrong architectural model.

02
Identity as the Billing Enforcement Layer

SaaS licenses attach to human identities, not hardware. Disconnected authentication and authorization systems permanently orphan licenses upon user deletion. The fix required formally connecting both via centralized permission profiles.

03
Federation as a Compliance Mandate, Not a Preference

SSO isn't a convenience feature; it's a legal IT mandate. Third-party credential storage violates corporate security policies. Federation had to be designed as foundational trust infrastructure, essential for procurement approval.

04
B2B CIAM Has No Established Playbook

Unlike consumer CIAM, B2B CIAM (hierarchies, delegated admin, bilateral agreements) lacks established design patterns. Solutions required original construction from first principles, heavily expanding the conceptual research scope.

05
The MSP Pattern — Formalize, Not Block

The dealer credential workaround wasn't just a security violation; it was an informal Managed Service Provider (MSP) model. Instead of blocking it, we formalized it into a secure, consent-based, auditable pathway (Module 04).

Research Synthesis

Six Structural Root Causes

500+ raw observations were clustered by shared root cause, not shared symptom. Six themes emerged — each representing a distinct architectural or behavioral failure the design needed to address at a structural level.

Artifact
[A2.1 Affinity Map Board — Full Observation Clusters]
Theme 01
Identity Formation is Broken

Unguarded self-registration produces isolated empty organizations. No mechanism routes users to the correct container at any stage of the process.

Drove the platform-wide registration intercept, domain-based organization discovery, and the request-to-join flow — making isolation structurally impossible rather than individually corrected.

Theme 02
Access Management is Architecturally Fragmented

Identity lived in myTopcon. Authorization lived in each application. No connecting layer. Every provisioning and offboarding event required manual navigation across disconnected systems.

Drove the centralized permission profile system, synchronizing identity and authorization in a single record — eliminating multi-app manual provisioning and making orphaned licenses structurally impossible.

Theme 03
No Digital Dealer-Customer Infrastructure

A commercial service relationship that functions in the real world has no representation in the system. Dealers operated inside customer environments using mechanisms designed for internal employees.

Drove the entire Module 04 architecture — bidirectional service agreements, scoped OAuth delegation, bilateral consent, tiered access levels, and instant org-level revocation.

Theme 04
Enterprise Scale is Unsupported

A single flat user list with no hierarchy cannot represent organizational structures enterprises actually use. Federation, domain verification, and divisional management were architecturally absent.

Drove divisional hierarchy in Module 03 and the domain verification and SSO configuration flows in Module 05 — the foundational infrastructure that makes enterprise IT procurement approval possible.

Theme 05
Self-Service Gaps Create Cascading Dependencies

Users cannot manage their own identity. Admins cannot transfer ownership. Every basic maintenance action creates a dependency on admin or engineering intervention downstream.

Drove the self-service account management panel in Module 02, the ownership transfer workflow in Module 05, and remote session management for field workers.

Theme 06
Operational Debt is Compounding

Each structural failure above generates recurring operational cost. Ticket volume grew 5× over five years, tracking SaaS expansion exactly. Engineering maintenance had no resolution state.

Provided the business case for treating Themes 01–05 as architectural requirements — the operational cost data established the urgency and scope that secured stakeholder alignment for full platform replacement.

Three Mental Models, Not Demographic Profiles

Personas were defined by operational goal and mental model — the internal logic users apply when the platform gives them an ambiguous signal. When platform structure matches mental model, it feels intuitive. When they conflict, every step requires deliberate effort.

Persona 01
Dealer Admin
Mental model: Portfolio Management
Core Goal

Verify client license → Fix issue → Move to next client. The unit of work is the customer organization, not the individual user.

Key Frictions
  • No centralized view of client onboarding status or license health
  • Context-switching errors from hidden active-organization indicators
  • Credential workarounds as the only path to establish client access
Persona 02
Customer Admin
Mental model: Infrastructure Control (Enterprise) · Total Delegation (SMB)
Core Goal

Maintain a secure, accurate software environment for the workforce — through self-management or full dealer delegation, depending on organizational size and maturity.

Key Frictions
  • Managing 200+ users across regions in a single flat list
  • Binary choice: block dealers entirely or grant full Admin equivalence
  • Manual permission tracking across disconnected application databases
Persona 03
End User
Mental model: Task Execution
Core Goal

Login → Application → Work. The platform is a gate, not a destination. Every step between authentication and the first application launch is friction to eliminate.

Key Frictions
  • Routed to an empty isolated organization by default after self-registration
  • No self-service path to correct profile errors or manage active sessions
  • Blocked from apps by concurrent session limits with no explanation

Planning

From Research to Blueprint

The synthesis produced a shared problem model. The planning phase translated it into architectural decisions, scope boundaries, and documentation contracts that structured everything built afterwards.

The Central Architectural Decision

The most consequential design decision in the project was made before any screen was designed: separating the personal Identity Space from the organizational Administration Space, connected by a persistent global context header. Every navigation and interaction decision across all five modules followed from this structural separation.

Decision
Problem It Solved
Rationale
Identity Space / Organization Space separation
The legacy system had no architectural separation between personal credential management and organizational administration — both existed in a single undifferentiated portal that was never rebuilt for SaaS-era complexity.
Matches the distinct mental models of personal identity ("what am I") versus organizational administration ("what do I manage"). Each space maintains a consistent experience regardless of the user's role in the other.
Customers in the dealer sidebar. Dealers in customer Settings.
Dealers and customers have an inverse relationship to the same subject. Treating both symmetrically in navigation misrepresents actual usage frequency for both sides.
For a Dealer Admin, managing customers is a daily operational task — primary navigation. For a Customer Admin, managing their dealer is an infrequent configuration task — Settings. Placement reflects frequency, not structural symmetry.
Requests separated from the active user directory
Mixing unverified access applicants with active organization members created conflicting interface actions and risked accidental license consumption before any review occurred.
Requests function as a gated staging area — reviewed and approved before anyone enters the directory or consumes a license. Reduces cognitive load and prevents premature resource allocation at any scale.
Divisions elevated to primary navigation
Treating organizational divisions as a Settings item buried a feature that enterprise admins interact with repeatedly as an operational target, not a one-time configuration.
Admins assign users and applications to divisions regularly. Operational targets belong in operational navigation. Settings is reserved for configuration that does not require frequent access.
