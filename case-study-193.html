The Problem

Compounding Failures on Two Fronts

Architectural limitations generated simultaneous pressure — operational costs for the business, and workflow failures for the people using the system daily.

Business Problems
User Problems

Architectural Mismatch

Authentication (myTopcon) and authorization (individual app databases) operated as disconnected systems. Deleting a user identity did not cascade to application licenses, permanently trapping paid software seats until engineering intervened manually.

Onboarding Dead Ends

Users registering without an invitation were placed in an isolated empty organization by default. No discovery path, no request mechanism, no route to their employer's actual environment without admin action.

No Digital Dealer Relationship

Topcon's commercial model relies on dealers as the primary customer interface. No formal digital structure existed for this relationship — no consent record, no audit trail, no scoped access, no revocation mechanism.

Dealer Credential Workarounds

The only available path for dealers to establish client access was a 17-step manual sequence involving creating accounts under the customer's own identity and self-assigning administrative rights. No formal alternative existed.

Enterprise Adoption Blocked

The platform had no federation capability. Enterprise IT departments require SSO integration with their own identity providers as a procurement condition, not a preference. Every enterprise contract stalling at this requirement was a blocked deal.

No Self-Service Controls

Users could not edit their own profiles, view active sessions, or remotely revoke device access. The organizational owner role was permanently tied to the original account creator — if they left, control was inaccessible without engineering intervention.

Database Fragmentation

Unverified free-text registration generated a new tenant for every company name variation typed. 14,800 zero-activity organizations accumulated, blocking automated billing infrastructure and requiring continuous engineering cleanup.

Flat Structure at Enterprise Scale

800 organizations each managing 200+ users were forced into a single undifferentiated list with no hierarchy, no divisions, and no structural separation between regions or business units.

The Solution

Separating "Me" from "We"

The legacy system collapsed personal identity management and organizational administration into a single undifferentiated interface. The new architecture makes a structural separation that resolves the core navigation and conceptual confusion for every user type.

Transformation Example — Module 04

From Credential Workaround to Formal Service Agreement

One workflow replaced entirely. The sharpest single illustration of what was built and why.

Before — Legacy Workaround
  • · Dealer creates account using the customer's own email address
  • · Dealer sets a placeholder password; customer notified by phone call
  • · Dealer invites own account as Admin; accepts from own inbox
  • · No consent record, no scope boundary, no expiry date
  • · Manual search-and-delete required to revoke each technician
After — Service Agreement Architecture
  • → Dealer initiates a formal service agreement request
  • → Customer reviews tiered access scope and provides bilateral consent
  • → Scoped OAuth token grants delegated, audited access
  • → Bilateral record — tier, scope, and consent timestamp
  • → Single-action org-level revocation terminates all dealer access

Chapter 02 — Discovery
A Two-Track Research Process

What to Build. How to Build It Correctly.

Research ran as two parallel streams with distinct purposes. Stakeholder-provided research established the mandates — the technical requirements, commercial pressures, and operational failures the design had to address. Independent UX research built the foundation to execute those mandates correctly — through domain knowledge, competitive interaction intelligence, and behavioral depth extracted through proxy research.

Track 01 — Stakeholder Research
Defined what needed to be built
Source 01
Business Context Analysis

Established the commercial pressure driving the project: the SaaS licensing shift, dealer network revenue dependency, and the four strategic outcomes required to secure investment.

Defined that federation, centralized permissions, a formalized dealer relationship, and scalable onboarding were non-negotiable commercial outcomes — not UX proposals. These became the project's fixed requirements before design involvement began.

Source 02
Quantitative Research

Confirmed structural failures at measurable scale: 46% single-member organizations, 310 orphaned licenses, 4,700 near-duplicate name records, and 12% of invitations never accepted.

Transformed anecdotal stakeholder concerns into undeniable evidence. Each number anchored a specific design decision to a documented structural failure rather than to an assumption or preference.

Source 03
Support Ticket Analysis

Documented 5× ticket growth tracking SaaS expansion directly. Two recurring categories — trapped licenses and ownership transfer — required engineering database intervention 100% of the time, every instance.

Proved that the recurring failures were architectural, not operational. No improved support workflow could resolve problems requiring direct database access by an engineer at every occurrence — automation was the only exit.

Source 04
Competitive Research Report

Trimble's 2021 migration established domain verification and centralized identity as the market baseline. Ping Identity was selected as the CIAM vendor. Competitors lacked formalized dealer access architecture.

Established the technical mandates (SAML/OIDC, domain verification) and identified the strategic gap Topcon could own: a formalized dealer-customer access model with no precedent in the industry's software platforms.

Track 02 — UX Investigation
Built the foundation to execute mandates correctly
Source 05
CIAM Industry Research

Built the domain vocabulary and conceptual framework needed to interpret stakeholder requirements accurately — and to avoid misdiagnosing structural failures as surface UI problems before synthesis began.

Produced five conceptual unlocks that reframed how the design problem was understood. Without them, each structural failure had a surface-level explanation that would have produced a wrong design response.

Source 06
Competitive UX Audit

Hands-on interaction testing across Trimble, Adobe, Atlassian, Microsoft Partner Center, Google Workspace, and AWS — evaluating provisioning flows, SSO configuration, domain verification, and partner access workflows.

Trimble's bypass-able registration warning, the hidden active-context problem observed across every tested platform, and Microsoft's unusable 100+ role consent model each produced specific UX decisions — not feature comparisons, but interaction intelligence.

Source 07
Internal Proxy Research

Extracted behavioral reality from internal stakeholders across three session formats — group sessions for mapping knowledge gaps, one-on-one sessions for operational depth, and written questions for precise sequence reconstruction.

The 17-step dealer credential workaround surfaced only in private sessions, weeks into the project, after trust built through repeated contact. Journey maps could not be built until this behavioral data arrived — the trust was a research precondition, not a side effect.

Why Domain Knowledge Was Non-Negotiable

Each structural failure in the legacy system had a surface-level explanation that would have led to a wrong design response. Ghost organizations look like a registration UI problem. Trapped licenses look like a database bug. The dealer workaround looks like a security incident to block. Without conceptual grounding built before synthesis began, the design would have addressed symptoms while leaving the architecture intact.

Conceptual Foundation

Five Unlocks That Reframed the Problem

The CIAM industry research produced five distinct shifts in how the design problem was understood. Each one changed what a correct design response looked like.

01
IAM vs. CIAM — The Wrong Architectural Model

The platform was built on enterprise IAM assumptions — bounded employee populations, organization-initiated relationships, high trust at entry. It was being exposed to CIAM behavior — self-registering external users, fluid boundaries, unverified identity. Ghost organizations were not bugs. They were the predictable output of applying the wrong model at scale.

Enterprise IAM manages known, contracted employees; the organization initiates every relationship. CIAM manages external users who initiate their own access with low trust at entry. Once this distinction was clear, every registration failure in the legacy system stopped looking like a UI problem and started looking like a structural certainty that needed an architectural response.

02
Identity as the Billing Enforcement Layer

In the hardware era, licenses attached to machine serial numbers — user identity was irrelevant to authorization. In SaaS, a license is a continuous authorization attached to a verified human identity. When authentication and authorization systems are disconnected, any deletion event in one permanently orphans what was attached in the other.

This reframe established why the trapped license problem was structural rather than incidental. It could not be fixed by improving the deletion workflow. It required authentication and authorization to be formally connected — which is exactly what the centralized permission profile achieves.

03
Federation as a Compliance Mandate, Not a Preference

Enterprise IT departments do not request SSO for user convenience. It is a legal compliance requirement: employee credentials must live exclusively in the corporate directory, and third-party credential storage violates the organization's own security policy. This established that federation was a non-negotiable procurement condition, not a premium feature.

This changed how the SSO configuration experience needed to be designed — not as an advanced settings screen, but as foundational trust infrastructure that enterprise IT requires before approving any procurement decision. The design had to treat it with that weight.

04
B2B CIAM Has No Established Playbook

Consumer CIAM has well-documented design patterns: social login, password recovery, individual registration flows. B2B CIAM — organizational hierarchies, delegated administration, multi-tenant context switching, bilateral service agreements — does not. Personas, information architecture, and service agreement flows could not be borrowed from precedent. They required original construction from first principles.

This shaped the project scope directly. Phases that would be routine on a consumer product required building conceptual foundations before execution could begin. Recognizing this early prevented the research and synthesis timeline from being scoped against the wrong reference point.

05
The MSP Pattern — Formalize, Not Block

The dealer credential workaround looked, from a security perspective, like a violation to close. The Managed Service Provider pattern in industry literature revealed it as something different: an informal version of a recognized B2B access model that the platform had never formally supported. The design response shifted from blocking the behavior to replacing it with a structured, auditable version that achieves the same commercial goal.

This reframe was the conceptual foundation for Module 4. Without it, the service agreement architecture would have been designed as a restriction mechanism. With it, it could be designed as a formal infrastructure that enables the same dealer-customer service relationship through a secure, consent-based, audit-separated pathway.

Chapter 03 — Planning

From Research to Blueprint

The synthesis of seven research streams produced a shared problem model. The planning phase translated that model into the architectural decisions, scope boundaries, and documentation contracts that structured everything built afterwards.

Synthesis — Affinity Map

Six Structural Root Causes

500+ raw observations were clustered by shared root cause, not shared symptom. Six themes emerged — each representing a distinct architectural or behavioral failure the design needed to address at a structural level.

Artifact A2.1
Affinity Map Board — Full Observation Clusters
Theme 01
Identity Formation is Broken

Unguarded self-registration produces isolated empty organizations. No mechanism routes users to the correct container at any stage of the process.

Drove the platform-wide registration intercept, domain-based organization discovery, and the request-to-join flow — making isolation structurally impossible rather than individually corrected.

Theme 02
Access Management is Architecturally Fragmented

Identity lived in myTopcon. Authorization lived in each app. No connecting layer. Every provisioning and offboarding event required manual navigation across disconnected systems.

Drove the centralized permission profile system, synchronizing identity and authorization in a single record — eliminating multi-app manual provisioning and making orphaned licenses structurally impossible.

Theme 03
No Digital Dealer-Customer Infrastructure

A commercial service relationship that functions in the real world has no representation in the system. Dealers operated inside customer environments using mechanisms designed for internal employees.

Drove the entire Module 04 architecture — bidirectional service agreements, scoped OAuth delegation, bilateral consent, tiered access levels, and instant org-level revocation.

Theme 04
Enterprise Scale is Unsupported

A single flat user list with no hierarchy cannot represent organizational structures enterprises actually use. Federation, domain verification, and divisional management were architecturally absent.

Drove divisional hierarchy in Module 03 and the domain verification and SSO configuration flows in Module 05 — the foundational infrastructure that makes enterprise IT procurement approval possible.

Theme 05
Self-Service Gaps Create Cascading Dependencies

Users cannot manage their own identity. Admins cannot transfer ownership. Every basic maintenance action creates a dependency on admin or engineering intervention downstream.

Drove the self-service account management panel in Module 02, the ownership transfer workflow in Module 05, and remote session management controls for field workers.

Theme 06
Operational Debt is Compounding

Each structural failure above generates recurring operational cost. Ticket volume grew 5× over five years, tracking SaaS expansion exactly. Engineering maintenance had no resolution state.

Provided the business case for treating Themes 01–05 as architectural requirements — the operational cost data established the urgency and scope that secured stakeholder alignment for full platform replacement.

Synthesis — Behavioral Personas

Three Mental Models, Not Demographic Profiles

Personas were defined by operational goal and mental model — the internal logic users apply when the platform gives them an ambiguous signal. When platform structure matches mental model, it feels intuitive. When they conflict, every step requires deliberate effort.

Persona 01
Dealer Admin
Mental model: Portfolio Management
Core Goal

Verify client license → Fix issue → Move to next client. The unit of work is the customer organization, not the individual user.

Key Frictions
  • No centralized view of client onboarding status or license health
  • Context-switching errors from hidden active-organization indicators
  • Credential workarounds as the only path to establish client access
Persona 02
Customer Admin
Mental model: Infrastructure Control (Enterprise) · Total Delegation (SMB)
Core Goal

Maintain a secure, accurate software environment for the workforce — through self-management or full dealer delegation, depending on organizational size and maturity.

Key Frictions
  • Managing 200+ users across multiple regions in a single flat list
  • Binary choice — block dealers entirely or grant full Admin equivalence
  • Manual permission tracking across disconnected application databases
Persona 03
End User
Mental model: Task Execution
Core Goal

Login → Application → Work. The platform is a gate, not a destination. Every step between authentication and the first application launch is friction to be eliminated.

Key Frictions
  • Routed to an empty isolated organization by default after self-registration
  • No self-service path to correct profile errors or manage active sessions
  • Blocked from apps by concurrent session limits with no explanation

Planning — Information Architecture

The Central Architectural Decision

The most consequential design decision in the project was made before any screen was designed: separating the personal Identity Space from the organizational Administration Space, connected by a persistent global context header. Every navigation and interaction decision in the five modules followed from this structural separation.

Decision Problem It Solved Rationale
Identity Space / Organization Space separation The legacy system mixed personal credentials with administrative controls in one interface, creating role-based confusion for every user type simultaneously. Matches the distinct mental models of personal identity ("what am I") versus organizational administration ("what do I manage"). Each space maintains a consistent experience regardless of the user's role in the other.
Customers in the dealer sidebar. Dealers in customer Settings. Dealers and customers have an inverse relationship to the same subject. Treating both symmetrically in navigation misrepresents actual usage frequency for both sides. For a Dealer Admin, managing customers is a daily operational task — primary navigation. For a Customer Admin, managing their dealer is an infrequent configuration task — Settings. Navigation placement reflects frequency, not structural symmetry.
Requests separated from the active user directory Mixing unverified access applicants with active organization members created conflicting interface actions and risked accidental license consumption before review. Requests function as a gated staging area — reviewed and approved before anyone enters the directory or consumes a license. Reduces cognitive load and prevents premature resource allocation at any scale.
Divisions elevated to primary navigation Treating organizational divisions as a Settings item buried a feature that enterprise admins interact with repeatedly as an operational target, not a one-time configuration. Admins assign users and applications to divisions regularly. Operational targets belong in operational navigation. Settings is reserved for infrequent configuration that does not require daily or weekly access.
